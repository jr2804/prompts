# SDO Writing Conventions

Extracted and generalized from: ITU English Language Style Guide (16 September 2024)

These mechanical writing conventions apply to formal standards documents
across all SDOs (3GPP, ETSI, ITU-T). ITU-specific examples are preserved
as the canonical source; the rules themselves are SDO-agnostic.

______________________________________________________________________

## 1. Written style

Documents must be written in clear, simple language without ambiguity.
Many readers are non-native English speakers; material is often translated.

______________________________________________________________________

## 2. Spelling

### Standard

Follow Oxford Premium Dictionaries (English, UK spelling). When alternatives
exist (e.g. "adviser (also advisor)"), use the first listed.

### -ize / -ise / -yse

- Prefer **-ize**: organize, liberalization, standardization, harmonize
- **-ise** when part of the root (not a suffix): surprise, comprise, enterprise,
  improvise, advertise, franchise
- **-yse** for Greek-derived words: analyse, dialyse, hydrolyse

### telecommunication(s)

- Noun: "telecommunications" (e.g. "The role of telecommunications")
- Adjective: "telecommunication" (e.g. "Telecommunication equipment")

### ICT(s)

- Full form: "information and communication technology/technologies"
  (NOT "communications")
- Abbreviation: "ICT" or "ICTs" as nouns; only "ICT" as adjective
  (e.g. "ICT applications", not "ICTs applications")

### Compounds

No hard-and-fast rules. General trend: consolidation over time
(e.g. radiocommunication, downlink, worldwide). Common compounds in Annex A.

### Plurals

- Prefer English plural forms for assimilated foreign words: forums, stadiums
- Context-dependent: antennae (insects) / antennas (radio); formulae
  (mathematical) / formulas (general); indices (mathematics) / indexes (books)
- "data" acceptable in both singular and plural

### Foreign words

- Italicize non-assimilated: inter alia, fait accompli, force majeure, per se
- Roman type for assimilated: ad hoc, vice versa, per capita, curriculum vitae

## 3. Hyphens

### General principle

Use hyphens only when necessary to avoid ambiguity or hesitation. Keep to
a minimum.

### Permanent hyphens

Some compounds have fixed hyphens: Secretary-General, time-limit, policy-maker.

### Prefixes

Most prefixes should NOT be hyphenated: subregion, multipath, semiconductor,
tripartite, bilateral, microcomputer, cybersecurity.

**Exceptions (always hyphenated):**

- **non-**, **self-**, **quasi-**, **ex-** (formerly): non-geostationary, self-sufficient
- Prefix ending with same vowel as root: pre-eminent, micro-organism
- To avoid awkward juxtapositions: co-worker
- To distinguish meanings: re-count (count again) vs recount (narrate)
- Before a capital letter: sub-Saharan, inter-American

**e- prefix**: always hyphenated: e-commerce, e-health, e-mail
**cyber prefix**: not hyphenated: cyberattack, cybersecurity
**tele prefix**: not hyphenated unless root starts with vowel: telemedicine,
telework; but tele-education

### Temporary hyphens (compound adjectives)

Use when attributive (before the noun):

- next-generation network, cost-benefit ratio, up-to-date information

**No hyphen** when:
a) First word is an adverb ending in -ly: readily available data
b) Expression derived from proper name: Latin American operators
c) Foreign-language expression: ad hoc group, ex officio member
d) Used predicatively: "the experts are better prepared" (vs "better-prepared experts")

## 4. Punctuation

### Oxford comma

Use where needed to avoid ambiguity: "ministries responsible for
telecommunications, trade, finance and budget, and industry"

### Spaces

Full stops, commas, colons, semicolons are followed (not preceded) by one space.
No spaces around hyphens: "time-sensitive content", "902-908 MHz".

______________________________________________________________________

## 5. Adjectives -- position affects meaning

| Before noun | After noun |
|-------------|------------|
| the concerned parents (= worried) | the parents concerned (= involved) |
| an involved argument (= complex) | the argument involved (= relevant) |
| the present situation (= current) | the people present (= here) |
| a responsible person (= reliable) | the person responsible (= in charge) |

In standards documents: "in collaboration with the study groups concerned"
(NOT "the concerned study groups").

## 6. Numbers

### General rule

Spell out whole numbers from zero to nine in body text. Use figures for:

- > = 10
- Before "million", "billion", "trillion"
- Dates, times, percentages, ratios, units of measurement, money
- Page references, serial numbers

### Spelling numbers

Hyphenate compound numbers above 20: twenty-three, two-thirds.

### Large numbers

No commas -- use spaces: 6 590 kHz, 1 500 assignments, 23 027 957 lines.
Exception: dates (1998) and serial numbers.

### Dates

Format: 17 January 1958. Avoid ambiguous abbreviated forms (02/10/94).
Spell out centuries: twentieth century. Decades: the early twenties, the late 1980s.
Time (24h): 2100 hours (no punctuation). Time (12h): 9 a.m., 1.15 p.m.

### Ranges

Either "13 360-13 410 kHz" or "from ... to ..." but never mixed:
NOT "from 6-10 May 1996".

### Decimals

Always precede decimal below unity with zero: 0.3 per cent, 0.5 deg E.
Use "per cent" in text (not %) except in tables for space reasons.

### Ordinals

Spell out up to "ninth"; figures from 10th onwards. "Eighth session, 14th session."

### Serial numbers

Use "No." or "Nos." for provisions: "No. 111 of the Radio Regulations".
Omit "No." after words like document, resolution, report in ITU texts.

## 7. Proper names

### Countries

Use official ITU/UN designations. Short forms in minutes:

- "Republic of Korea" (not South Korea)
- "Democratic People's Republic of Korea" (not North Korea)
- "Russian Federation", "Islamic Republic of Iran", "Syrian Arab Republic"

Use definite article where grammar dictates: "from the Republic of Croatia".

### Organizations

Follow the organization's own English spelling and hyphenation, even if it
conflicts with standard conventions.

### ITU conferences

Format: Full name (City, Year). Abbreviation: WRC-19, WTSA-16, PP-98.

## 8. Capitalization

### General rule

Lower case for general references. Initial capitals ONLY for specific,
numbered references.

| General (lower case) | Specific (initial caps) |
|---|---|
| ...to be submitted to the next plenipotentiary conference | the Plenipotentiary Conference (Busan, 2014) |
| ...adopted several resolutions | ...adopted Resolution 51 |

"When in doubt, use lower case."

### Always capitalized

Administration, Government, Member State, Sector Member, the Council,
the Constitution, the Convention, the Radio Regulations, the Bureau,
the Secretary-General, Deputy Secretary-General.

### Never capitalized (unless grammar requires)

session, delegate, delegation, observer, budget, strategic plan, study period,
information society, least developed country (LDC).

### Recommendation and Question

Capitalized when referring to ITU Sectors' formal standards: "ITU-T
Recommendation P.1401", "Question 9/12". Lower case in PP output texts.

## 9. Abbreviations and acronyms

### General

- Spell out on first appearance: "information and communication technologies (ICTs)"
- Do not abbreviate in titles of official texts
- Provide abbreviation list for long documents
- Two-letter abbreviations are often ambiguous; avoid in general

### Plurals

Add lowercase "s": ICTs, LDCs (NOT LDC's or LDCS).

### Articles

- Omit definite article before org abbreviations: "ITU", not "the ITU"
- Indefinite article depends on pronunciation: a VoIP device, an LDC

### Common forms

| Singular | Plural |
|----------|--------|
| Ref. [1] | Refs [1, 2] |
| Fig. 1 | Figs 3 and 4 |
| Vol. 1 | Vols 7-9 |
| Eq. (5) | Eqs (5, 6) |
| No. 123 | Nos. 123 and 124 |
| p. 1 | pp. 1-9 |
| para. 1 | paras. 2-4 |

"e.g." and "i.e." are not followed by a comma; "vs" has no full stop.

## 10. Layout

### Paragraph numbering

Limit to whole numbers or one decimal place: 1, 1.1, 2.1. Avoid deep nesting
like 3.21.2.41.

### Titles and headings

- Bold type (no underlining)
- Initial capital on first word only: "Preliminary draft plan of action"
- Composite headings: capital on each part: "The changing telecommunication
  environment: Ways and means of enhancing the Union's relevance"

### Internal cross-references

Use **"clause"** (not "chapter" or "section") when referring to parts of
an ITU-T Recommendation or contribution document. Examples:

- "as described in clause 8" (NOT "chapter 8")
- "see clause 7.2" (NOT "section 7.2")
- "according to clause 10.5.2" (NOT "section 10.5.2")

"Chapter" and "section" are editorial artefacts from word processors, not
part of the ITU-T document model. Use "clause" consistently in all internal
cross-references.

### Lists -- three formats (be consistent within a document)

**Format A** (items < 1 sentence): lower case, full stop only on last item

```
The purpose of training is to:
- improve staff qualifications
- meet the organization's human resources needs
- keep abreast of new technologies.
```

**Format B** (longer items): semicolons between items, full stop on last

```
The purposes were to:
- adopt new and revised Questions;
- adopt regional initiatives; and
- agree on the strategic plan.
```

**Format C** (each item >= 1 sentence): capitals, full stops on all

```
The evaluation should include certain steps:
- All those involved decide jointly to use a participatory approach.
- When agreement is reached, a small group is elected.
- The best methods are then chosen.
```

### Neutral order

In official documents, use prescribed neutral orders:

- Countries: French alphabetical order (by long name)
- Languages: English, Arabic, Chinese, Spanish, French, Russian
- ITU Sectors: ITU-R, ITU-T, ITU-D

### "etc."

Always preceded by a comma. Do not use with "e.g.", "including",
"for example", or "such as" -- these already indicate non-exhaustiveness.

## 11. Non-discriminatory language

### Gender-neutral

- Terms for functions are gender neutral: chair, delegate, observer, Director
- Use neutral terms where possible: "spouse" not "wife"/"husband"
- Avoid masculine-only pronouns:
  - Delete: "The trainee is usually the best judge of the training"
  - Use plural: "Trainees are often the best judge of their training"
  - Rephrase: "The best judge of the training is often the trainee"
- Prefer "staff" over "man", "workforce" over "manpower",
  "supervisor" over "foreman"
- Use "chair" (not "chairman") for the function/role

### Disability-inclusive

- People-first language: "persons with disabilities", "persons with specific needs"
- Avoid defining people solely by disability: "blind persons" not "the blind"
- Neutral, non-suffering language: "has dyslexia" not "suffers from dyslexia";
  "wheelchair user" not "wheelchair-bound"
- "living with HIV" not "HIV-infected"
